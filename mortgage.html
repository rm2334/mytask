A mortgage is a legal document between a you, the borrower, and a financial firm, like a mortgage company or bank. The mortgage pledges the property as security until the loan amount is paid in full. The total amount of the loan is the purchase price plus interest and any fees charged for setting up and managing the loan.

The borrower agrees to pay back the loan in a specific length of time, with payments that are either fixed for the term of the loan or that vary based on market conditions. At the end of the loan period, if all amounts have been paid, you own the property, free and clear.

FHA Mortgages

FHA (Federal Housing Authority) loans: Limited to homebuyers who do not qualify for a market-rate loan. An FHA loan is serviced by a conventional mortgage lender. The FHA borrower pays a premium upfront at closing and as a monthly fee, but financial qualifications, down payments and other terms can be more liberal than market loans

Same for the lifetime of the loan

Jumbo Loan Mortgages

Most home loans have limits on the amount that can be borrowed. A Jumbo Loan is a mortgage that packages a conventional ‘conforming’ loan with a second loan for the amount over the limit. The current US national limit is $417,000; it is $625,000 in high-cost real estate markets. Since the total amount of the loan is large, Jumbo loans can have a higher interest rate.

Interest Rate:

Same for the lifetime of the loan

Payment:

Same for the lifetime of the loan

Term:

15- and 30-year loans are the most common

  • Allows you to purchase a higher-priced home

Second Mortgages

To get additional cash, you can take out a second loan using your home as collateral. Second mortgages usually have a higher interest rate, since the first mortgage gets paid off first if you default. Second mortgages may require that you have built up equity by paying off a significant portion of the first mortgage

Interest rate:

The same for the lifetime of the loan

Payment:

Can be structured as a fixed amount paid for the term of the loan (home equity term); or as a fixed amount that omits some of the accrued interest then has a ‘balloon’ payment at the end of the loan term

Term:

Varies, but is typically equal to or less than the remaining term of the first mortgage

  • A popular method to pay for home renovations, college, debt consolidation and other financial needs

Portfolio Mortgages

In many cases, lenders package a group of mortgages and sell them in the financial market. With a portfolio mortgage, your lender believes you are a good credit risk and keeps your mortgage as part of the firm’s investment portfolio. Customer service is generally higher on a portfolio mortgage.

Interest Rate:

Often has a low introductory rate, followed by a higher rate for the remainder of the loan period

Payment:

Fixed payment for the introductory period, followed by a new fixed rate for the remainder of the term

Term:

15- and 30-year loans are the most common, with a lower-rate introductory period of two to five years

  • If you have great credit, a portfolio loan can mean attractive rates, waived insurance and superior service
